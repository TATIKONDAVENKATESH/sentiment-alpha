# Bitcoin Market Sentiment vs. Hyperliquid Trader Behavior & Performance

## 1. Problem Statement

Does Bitcoin market sentiment (Fear & Greed) relate to how traders behave and how
profitable they are on Hyperliquid? This project explores that relationship end-to-end:
data cleaning, merging, feature engineering, exploratory analysis, statistical testing,
trader segmentation, risk analysis, and actionable strategy insights.

## 2. Objective

1. Determine how trader performance changes across market sentiment conditions.
2. Determine how trading behavior changes across sentiment conditions.
3. Identify hidden patterns in profitability, risk, position sizing, trade frequency,
   and trade direction.
4. Compare successful and unsuccessful traders.
5. Statistically validate key observations.
6. Translate findings into actionable trading-strategy insights.

## 3. Datasets

| Dataset | Rows | Columns | Date range |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Bitcoin Fear & Greed Index** (`fear_greed_index.csv`) | 2,644 | `timestamp`, `value`, `classification`, `date` | 2018-02-01 to 2025-05-02 |
| **Hyperliquid Historical Trades** (`historical_data.csv`) | 211,224 | `Account`, `Coin`, `Execution Price`, `Size Tokens`, `Size USD`, `Side`, `Timestamp IST`, `Start Position`, `Direction`, `Closed PnL`, `Transaction Hash`, `Order ID`, `Crossed`, `Fee`, `Trade ID`, `Timestamp` | 2023-05-01 to 2025-05-01 |

- 32 unique trader accounts, 246 unique traded assets in the trading dataset.
- **Important data-quality finding**: the raw `Timestamp` (epoch ms) column in the trade
  data is rounded to only 7 distinct values across all 211k rows — a precision-loss
  artifact in the source export. `Timestamp IST` (minute precision) was used instead as
  the authoritative time field for all date/time analysis.
- No `Leverage` column exists in the source data, so all leverage-based analysis was
  skipped rather than fabricated.

## 7. Methodology

- **Cleaning**: column standardization, timestamp parsing (with the precision-loss issue
  above documented and handled), sentiment classification standardization, duplicate
  checks (0 found), and explicit non-removal of financial outliers.
- **Merge**: trades joined to sentiment on calendar date (1:1, no row duplication).
  211,218 of 211,224 rows (100.00%) matched a sentiment label; only 6 rows on one date
  (2024-10-26) were unmatched.
- **Feature engineering**: `is_profitable`/`is_loss` flags computed only for *closing*
  trades (closed_pnl != 0); opening fills carry PnL=0 by construction and are excluded
  from win-rate/PnL comparisons. Safe ratios (fee/size, PnL/size) guard against
  divide-by-zero on zero-size "dust conversion" rows.
- **Trader-level threshold**: a minimum of 30 closing trades was required for a trader to
  be included in comparative segment analysis (all 32 traders met this bar).
- **Statistics**: Kruskal-Wallis (PnL across 5 sentiment groups, non-parametric because
  PnL is heavy-tailed), Mann-Whitney U (top vs. bottom trader segments), Chi-square
  (sentiment vs. profitability, with Cramer's V effect size), and Spearman correlation
  (trade frequency vs. performance, size vs. PnL) — all with alpha=0.05, explicit H0/H1,
  and plain-English interpretation.
- **Robustness**: outlier concentration (top-trader/top-trade PnL share) and winsorized
  (1st/99th percentile) re-computation of sentiment performance, to check whether
  conclusions hold when extreme values are dampened.

## 8. Key Findings (real, calculated values)

- **Total PnL** is highest during **Fear** ($3,357,155) and lowest during **Extreme Fear**
  ($739,110).
- **Median PnL** tells a different story: **Extreme Greed** has the highest median closed
  PnL ($8.53/trade) vs. Fear's median of just $6.35 — total PnL during Fear is driven by a
  smaller number of large winning trades, not a systematically better typical trade.
- **Win rate** is highest in **Extreme Greed** (89.2%) and lowest in **Extreme Fear**
  (76.2%).
- **Risk-adjusted performance** (mean PnL ÷ PnL std dev) is highest in **Extreme Greed**
  (0.123) and lowest in **Extreme Fear** (0.044) — Fear's large total PnL comes with
  materially more volatility per unit of return than Extreme Greed's.
- A **Kruskal-Wallis test** confirmed PnL distributions differ significantly across the 5
  sentiment categories (H = 730.33, p ≈ 9.4×10⁻¹⁵⁷).
- **Trading frequency is highest during Extreme Fear** (47.8 trades/trader/day) and lowest
  during Greed (8.4) — traders in this dataset are *most* active during panic, not hype,
  the opposite of a naive "FOMO buying" narrative.
- **BUY/SELL mix barely shifts with sentiment** (44.9%-51.1% BUY share across all 5
  categories) — no strong directional bias emerges just from sentiment.
- A **Chi-square test** confirmed sentiment and trade profitability are significantly
  associated (Cramer's V ≈ 0.14 — a real but modest effect size).
- **Top 20% traders (by total PnL) trade far more often** (250.4 trades/active day) than
  Bottom 20% traders (98.5/day) and pay much higher fees ($18,765 vs. $6,062 on average) —
  but their win-rate edge (84.7% vs. 75.4%) was **not statistically significant**
  (Mann-Whitney p = 0.097) given only 32 traders total.
- **Concentration**: the top 5 traders account for **61.8%** of all realized PnL, and the
  top 1% of trades by absolute PnL account for **48.8%** of total absolute PnL — a small
  number of traders/trades dominate the results, so headline averages should be read with
  caution.
- **BUY vs. SELL PnL gaps by sentiment shrink or reverse once the top 5% of extreme trades
  are excluded** (e.g., Extreme Fear's BUY-SELL gap flips from +$69 to -$21) — apparent
  direction-sentiment patterns are largely driven by a handful of extreme trades, not a
  broad-based effect.
- A **winsorized robustness check** confirmed Extreme Greed remains the top sentiment by
  mean PnL before and after clipping extreme values — this particular conclusion is
  robust, even though total-PnL rankings are not.

## 10. Statistical Methods Used

| Test | Used for | Result |
|---|---|---|
| Kruskal-Wallis | Closed PnL across 5 sentiment groups | H=730.33, p≈9.4e-157 (significant) |
| Chi-square + Cramer's V | Sentiment vs. profitability | Chi2=1976.45, p≈0, Cramer's V=0.138 (significant, modest effect) |
| Mann-Whitney U | Win rate, Top 20% vs. Bottom 20% traders | U=38.0, p=0.097 (not significant) |
| Spearman correlation | Trade frequency vs. PnL/trade, fees, win rate; size vs. PnL | see `run_log.txt` for all rho/p values |

## 11. Limitations

- Only 32 trader accounts limits statistical power for trader-segment comparisons.
- No leverage data available; leverage-based risk analysis was not performed.
- Raw millisecond timestamps were unusable due to source rounding; minute-precision
  `Timestamp IST` was used instead.
- 246 raw asset codes include unmapped numeric tickers (e.g. `@107`) that could not be
  resolved to real asset names from the data provided.
- Daily sentiment is a single macro signal applied uniformly to all trades on that date
  and cannot capture intraday sentiment shifts.

## 12. Future Improvements

- Obtain an asset-ticker mapping table to resolve numeric coin codes.
- Incorporate leverage data if it becomes available.
- Expand the trader sample to increase statistical power for segment comparisons.
- Add intraday sentiment proxies (funding rates, order-book imbalance) to complement the
  daily Fear & Greed Index.
